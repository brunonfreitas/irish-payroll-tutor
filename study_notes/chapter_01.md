# Chapter 1 - Introduction to Employment Law

## Areas
Employment Law, WRC, Labour Court, Civil Law, Criminal Law, Compliance, Statutory Instruments, REA, SEO, Fines Act

## Key Points
- Employment law is Civil Law — the objective is redress (compensation), not punishment
- Criminal Law applies only in specific cases: Health & Safety breaches and Protection of Young Persons Act 1996
- Primary legislation = Acts passed by the Dáil; Secondary legislation = Statutory Instruments (SIs)
- Legislation sets the legal minimum — rights can be varied upwards but NEVER downwards
- Employment law applies to employees only — self-employed have NO protection whatsoever
- Since the Workplace Relations Act 2015, there are only 2 bodies: WRC and Labour Court (previously 5)
- WRC handles complaints, inspections, mediation and adjudication
- Complaints to WRC must be made within 6 months (extendable to 12 with reasonable cause)
- Employer must comply with Adjudication Officer decision within 56 days (8 weeks)
- Employer must comply with Labour Court determination within 42 days
- Compliance Notice can only be issued for breach of an Act — NOT a Statutory Instrument (Boots Retail v WRC)
- Fixed Payment Notice is an on-the-spot fine — maximum €2,000, pay within 42 days to avoid prosecution
- Fines Act 2010: Class A = €5,000 | Class B = €4,000 | Class C = €2,500 | Class D = €1,000 | Class E = €500
- Summary offence maximum prison sentence = 12 months (District Court, no jury)
- REA (Registered Employment Agreement) applies only to the parties who signed it
- SEO (Sectoral Employment Order) also called ERO — applies to ALL workers in an entire economic sector
- Bogus self-employment = classifying an employee as self-employed to avoid PRSI and employment rights — Revenue investigates actively
- PAYE is deducted before the employee receives net pay — it is a pay-as-you-earn system operated by Revenue
- Tax credits reduce the amount of PAYE payable — they are allocated by Revenue via the RPN
- Revenue is the Irish tax authority — it controls PAYE, PRSI (in part), USC and all employer reporting obligations

## Civil Law vs. Criminal Law — Full Comparison

| | Civil Law | Criminal Law |
|---|---|---|
| Objective | Protect individual rights — redress (compensation) | Protect society — punish the offender |
| Standard of proof | Balance of probabilities (~51%) | Beyond reasonable doubt (~99%) |
| Result | Financial compensation | Fine and/or imprisonment |
| Who prosecutes | The injured individual | The State |
| Employment law examples | Payment of Wages Act breach, unpaid leave | Health & Safety breach, employing minors illegally |

## Sources of Employment Law

### Primary Legislation (Acts — passed by the Dáil)
Key Acts relevant to payroll:
- Payment of Wages Act 1991 — payslips and deductions
- Organisation of Working Time Act 1997 — annual leave, rest breaks, working hours
- Protection of Young Persons (Employment) Act 1996 — employing minors

### Secondary Legislation — Statutory Instruments (SIs)
Regulations that detail how primary legislation is applied in practice.
Example: SI No. 473 of 2001 — records required under the Organisation of Working Time Act 1997.

**Critical rule:** A Compliance Notice CANNOT be issued for a breach of a Statutory Instrument — only for a breach of an Act. (Case Law: Boots Retail (Ireland) Ltd v WRC — CNN194)

### Fundamental Principle
Legislation sets the legal minimum. Rights can be varied upwards by agreement, but NEVER downwards — even with employee consent.

- Giving more than the statutory minimum → always lawful
- Giving less than the statutory minimum → always unlawful, even if employee agrees
- Example: statutory minimum annual leave = 4 weeks. Giving 6 weeks = lawful. Giving 3 weeks = unlawful even with written consent.

## The Dispute Resolution System

### Structure Since Workplace Relations Act 2015

```
EMPLOYEE submits complaint to WRC (within 6 months)
         ↓
    WRC — Workplace Relations Commission
    ├── Mediation Service (voluntary — both parties must consent)
    └── Adjudication Officer issues decision
              ↓ appeal within 42 days
         LABOUR COURT (legally binding determination)
              ↓ appeal on point of law only
           HIGH COURT (final)
```

### WRC — Four Main Functions
1. Information — provides information on employment law, equality and industrial relations
2. Advisory & Conciliation — helps resolve disputes voluntarily before formal adjudication
3. Inspection — inspectors visit workplaces and review payroll records for compliance
4. Adjudication — Adjudication Officers hear and decide formal cases

### Labour Court — Key Roles
- Hears ALL appeals of Adjudication Officer decisions (sole appellate jurisdiction since 2015)
- Issues legally binding determinations
- Registers REAs and establishes SEOs
- Can refer questions of law to the High Court
- Decisions can only be appealed to the High Court on a point of law (not facts)

## REA vs. SEO — Full Comparison

| | REA (Registered Employment Agreement) | SEO (Sectoral Employment Order) |
|---|---|---|
| Who negotiates | One employer + trade union | Representative trade union or employer body for a whole sector |
| Who it applies to | Only the parties who signed it | ALL employers and employees in the sector — regardless of involvement |
| Also known as | — | ERO (Employment Regulation Order) |
| Registered with | Labour Court | Labour Court (recommendation to Minister) |
| Minister's role | None | Must accept recommendation within 6 weeks |

## Fines Act 2010 — Classes of Fines

| Class | A | B | C | D | E |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Maximum Fine | €5,000 | €4,000 | €2,500 | €1,000 | €500 |

Memory tip: A = largest (€5,000) → E = smallest (€500). Each step down roughly halves the value.

- Summary offence (District Court, no jury) → maximum prison: **12 months**
- Conviction on indictment (with jury) → maximum prison: **3 years** (e.g. non-compliance with a Compliance Notice)

## Employee vs. Self-Employed — Critical for Payroll

Employment law applies to employees only. Revenue uses the *Code of Practice for Determining Employment Status* to classify workers — similar in concept to Brazil's CLT vínculo empregatício criteria.

### Indicators of Employee Status
- Works for one employer only
- Follows hours and schedule set by the employer
- Uses employer's equipment and tools
- Receives a fixed salary regardless of results
- Cannot subcontract their work to another person
- Integrated into the organisational structure

### Indicators of Self-Employed Status
- Works for multiple clients
- Sets their own hours and schedule
- Uses their own equipment
- Bears financial risk (can make profit or loss)
- Can subcontract work to others
- Issues invoices for services rendered

### Brazil vs. Ireland Comparison

| | Brazil (CLT) | Ireland |
|---|---|---|
| Criteria | 5 elements: pessoalidade, onerosidade, habitualidade, subordinação, não eventualidade | Code of Practice — holistic analysis of multiple factors |
| Who decides | Tribunal do Trabalho | Revenue / WRC / Courts |
| Risk for employer | Reconhecimento de vínculo + FGTS + verbas rescisórias | PAYE + PRSI arrears + interest + Revenue penalties |
| Term for the problem | Pejotização | Bogus self-employment |

**Payroll impact:** If Revenue determines a "contractor" was actually an employee, the company must pay all outstanding PAYE and PRSI with interest and penalties. The payroll specialist will be required to reconstruct the full payment history.

## Payroll Context — Revenue, PAYE and Tax Credits

Understanding these three concepts from Chapter 1 is the foundation for all payroll calculations covered in later chapters:

- **Revenue** is the Irish tax authority — equivalent to the Brazilian Receita Federal. It controls PAYE collection, USC, employer PRSI reporting and all payroll compliance obligations. Every employer in Ireland must register with Revenue and report payroll in real time via the PAYE Modernisation system (in place since January 2019).
- **PAYE (Pay As You Earn)** is the system by which income tax is deducted from an employee's gross pay before they receive their net pay. The employer deducts PAYE on behalf of Revenue and remits it monthly. The employee never handles the tax directly.
- **Tax Credits** reduce the amount of PAYE an employee must pay. They are not deductions from income — they are deductions from the tax itself. Revenue allocates tax credits to each employee via the **RPN (Revenue Payroll Notification)**, which the employer must retrieve before processing payroll.

## Common Interview Traps — Chapter 1

1. "Can an employee waive their right to a payslip?" → NO. Payment of Wages Act 1991 is mandatory — no contracting out.
2. "Can a Compliance Notice be issued for a Statutory Instrument breach?" → NO. Acts only. (Boots Retail v WRC)
3. "Does a SEO only apply to companies that took part in negotiations?" → NO. It applies to the entire sector.
4. "Is a breach of the Payment of Wages Act a criminal offence?" → NO. It is Civil Law.
5. "Does self-employment give limited protection under some Acts?" → NO. Zero protection — none at all.
6. "What is the maximum prison sentence for a summary offence?" → 12 months — NOT 2 or 3 years.
7. "Do tax credits increase gross pay?" → NO. Tax credits reduce the PAYE liability — they do not affect gross pay.
8. "Does the employee pay PAYE directly to Revenue?" → NO. The employer deducts and remits PAYE on behalf of the employee.
